Syllabus

Much of today’s software engineering aims to automate the process of understanding software artifacts. This graduate class focuses on techniques that analyze computer programs for purposes such as finding security vulnerabilities and errors, or understanding the software development process.

Our goal is to give you a working knowledge of program analysis and its theoretical foundations rooted in abstract interpretation. After taking this class you should be able to read and understand most papers in the field.

The class is split in two parts. The first part consists of lectures on key concepts of program analysis including: data flow analysis and abstract interpretation; applications to security, bug finding and testing; dynamic analysis; and machine-learning based approaches that tradeoff soundness in favor of scalability. The second part of the class focuses on research papers. Your mission, if you choose to accept it, is to reproduce one paper’s results.

Workload and Grading

This is an advanced class designed so you can choose how much you want to invest in the class. My expectation is that most of you will spend about a day a week. Most of the work is reading and writing. There are opportunities for programming in the second half of the class, but only if you choose a reanalysis or a reproduction. I expect the deliverables to be:

  • Assignments: questions on course material. (3x)
  • Midterm: same as assignments, in-class, on October 23. (1x)
  • Reviews: a one page review of a research paper. (2x)
  • Presentation: a lecture on your chosen research paper. Done in group. (1x)
  • Project: a blog post describing the outcome of your reproduction and a presentation. Done in group. (1x)

The project is the only only open-ended task here.

Grades are somewhat besides the point in a research class, but they are required by the college. My expectation is that the range of final grades will be B, B+, A- and A, where the semantics of the letter grades are that B is a below average contribution, B+ and A- mean you met expectations and A+ means you exceeded expectations.

Project

The project is the emphasis of the second half of the course. Working in groups of up to 3 students, you will select a research paper, obtain its data and code from the authors, and perform a reproduction study. You will present the paper in class, and then report on your reproduction.

Reproduction study

There are three alternatives: repetition, reanalysis, or reproduction. Detail descriptions of those terms are in this paper. A repetition attempts to establish that given the code and data provided by the authors, the results of the paper hold. Reanalysis starts from the author’s code and data, but attempts to evaluate if the results are robust in the presence of changes to the methodology. Reproduction attempt to reimplement some part of the approach and determine if the same conclusions can still be obtained.

Report

Your report should do the following: (1) summarize the paper’s approach and results, (2) describe how you validated the results, and (3) summarize what you found. In simple cases, when everything is as claimed, the report can be quite short (a couple of pages), but if there are interesting differences the reports can be longer. It is conceivable that some of the reports may be extended into papers after the end of class.

Lecture

Each group will prepare a lecture on their paper. The lecture is 90 minutes long including time for questions and discusion. The goal of the lecture is to give an in-depth presentation of the paper, placing it in context of previous research and talk about is strenghs and limitation.

Assignments

We will have 3 in-class assignments, each one will consist of about 3 questions from the textbook.

Papers

Here are some ideas for papers to reproduce. We are looking for papers that come with software and data. Getting those may require interacting with the authors. You can choose other papers, e.g. from recent proceedings of conferences such as ICSE, FSE, ASE and OOPSLA.

  • Do Automatically Generated Unit Tests Find Real Faults? ASE’15
